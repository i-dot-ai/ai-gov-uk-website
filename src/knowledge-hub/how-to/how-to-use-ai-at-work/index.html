How to use AI at work (draft)

Back to top
! Warning This is not a comprehensive guide, but a starting point to support your AI journey. This is not intended to be any form of legal guidance or advice, and should not be used as a substitute for independent legal advice.

AI assistants (such as, Microsoft Copilot, Gemini, and Claude) can help with your work, if you use them responsibly. They work by taking your question or instruction (a prompt) and predicting the most likely response.

The quality of AI output depends on how you write the prompt, and the information used to train the tool. It may not give you what you want, and you may get a different response if you ask the same question again.

How to get the best from AI

You will get the best results if you treat AI as an assistant, and talk to it in a conversational way. For example, ask it:

  • to explain why it made a decision step by step
  • for any assumptions it made
  • to check its work and suggest alternative options
  • to list all sources of information and verbatim quotes used

Write your prompts as specifically and simply as possible, including giving context and examples. Ask it to be critical, to question assumptions, and to highlight anything it’s not certain about. Learn more about how to experiment with prompts.

Use markdown formatting to help emphasise instructions in a way AI will understand. For example:

  • use asterix’s as bullet points
  • use capitals or an asterix either side of a word for emphasis (for example, ‘you MUST give sources’)
  • use // to split up long prompts (for example, ‘// Goal’)

Improve your results by asking the AI to review your prompts before you submit them (meta prompting). For example, ‘review and improve this prompt. Ask me questions if anything's unclear’.

How to check AI responses

You're responsible for checking any AI response before you use it, to make sure it's accurate and relevant. Use your knowledge and judgement to check if the output makes sense for your specific situation. Ask yourself:

  • does this match what I know about the topic?
  • are there any obvious errors or gaps?
  • does it make sense for my audience?
  • would I be comfortable putting my name to this?

Review any important or sensitive outputs with an expert, or somebody who knows the subject well.

Limitations of AI

The information you give to the AI will influence the quality of the response.

If you give the AI specific source material, such as a document or pasted text, the response will be more reliable.

If you use a short prompt or ask the AI to create new content without any source material, the response is less trustworthy. This is because you do not know what information the AI is using, and it may invent details.

Always review AI’s responses, as it may:

  • give wrong or misleading information (hallucinate)
  • miss important information
  • not understand the context
  • be biased
  • use offensive language
  • not follow accessibility rules

To make it easier to check the AI's accuracy, you should:

  • ask it to give sources and use direct quotes from them
  • use your AI tool's research function if it has one - this searches for and cites sources
AI can create fake sources or misquote real ones. Always check that sources exist and say what the AI claims.

When to use AI

Only use AI when it adds value, as there are high environmental and financial costs. Consider if there are other tools you could use instead. Learn how to use AI ethically and sustainably.

Working with existing content

AI is good at working with existing content, for example, you can upload as a document or paste content into an assistant. This reduces the chance of it giving wrong information. For example, you can use it to:

  • suggest spelling and grammar corrections
  • summarise reports or documents
  • find common themes in multiple documents
  • create presentations or discussion points from reports

Researching new information

You can use AI to create new information, for example, review external content, research new information or explain ideas. There is a higher chance of wrong answers due to hallucinations, bias or lack of context.

If you use it for research you must review the content to make sure the results are accurate, unbiased and based on reliable sources.

When not to use AI

You must not use AI assistants to replace human judgement or in a misleading way, for example:

  • changing meaning
  • making decisions
  • sifting job applications
  • simulating real people

How to use AI in government

Always follow the Civil service code and use them with integrity, honesty and objectivity. Government departments can access different AI assistants. They have different strengths and weaknesses, but all are generally suitable for general government tasks.

Do not use any free AI tools for government work, as information is used to train models. Only use tools paid for and approved by your department

Review and approve AI-generated content

If you use AI to create or change official content, you must follow the ‘Human-in-the-Loop requirement’. You must review the content and sign-off its use for any official reason (for example, a report or web content).

You must also state when AI has been used to create or edit official content.

You do not need to do this for personal content (such as emails) but it’s best practice to review any content created or edited by AI.

Do not use sensitive information

You may have access to personal or sensitive information (for example, email addresses, names or phone numbers). You must anonymise any information you use in prompts or uploads, (for example, change ‘John Smith’ to ‘Person A’).

AI content and Freedom of Information

AI content can be requested under the Freedom of Information Act, for example:

  • minutes of meetings using AI transcription
  • prompts and responses
  • presentations

Ask for consent if you are using AI assistants in group situations (for example, when transcribing minutes).
